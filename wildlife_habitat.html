UNDERSTANDING THE KALAHARI

The word ‘desert’ originally meant ‘an abandoned place’ rather than a very dry one. Tswalu doesn’t fit either mould thanks to the local mountains, it receives more rainfall than the rest of the Kalahari and is an astonishingly diverse and fecund habitat.

The only mountain range in the southern Kalahari – the Koranneberg – casts a benevolent shadow over the rolling dunes and grasslands of Tswalu. They also gift Tswalu more rainfall than more arid regions, which turn makes the reserve unusually fertile. In the shelter of the mountains – which also provide a dramatic backdrop to wildlife sightings – you can encounter Tswalu’s 240 species of bird and 80 mammal species, including the Critically Endangered desert-adapted black rhino and Endangered wild dog.

SPEND TIME WITH RARE SPECIES

Tswalu offers you the opportunity to view species you may have seen elsewhere, side-by-side with arid area specialists that you probably haven’t. While zebra, buffalo and giraffe will be familiar if you’ve been on safari before, this will likely be your first opportunity to capture iconic Kalahari images such as dainty springbok with a backdrop of red sand, or the silhouette of a gemsbok cresting a dune. Many of the species found at Tswalu are of particular significance to the San people, as evidenced by their inclusion in the ancient galleries of rock art at sacred sites.

There is a timeless quality to Kalahari – it’s a place where humans have wandered and wondered since the dawn of creation.

We stay true to this feeling by letting you shape your own days and spending as long as you wish on each activity. Just like the sands of the Kalahari, we understand that your priorities can shift during your time at Tswalu. That’s why we’ll never try and shoehorn your experience into a fixed schedule.

FAST CATS AND ANT BEARS

Tswalu is unquestionably one of the best places in southern Africa to see cheetah in the wild – the vast open spaces and antelope herds of the Kalahari readily lend themselves to high-speed pursuits. Visiting Tswalu also gives you front-row seats for sightings of species that prove elusive elsewhere, from pangolin to Hartmann’s Mountain Zebra.

MIND YOUR MANORS

Their gregarious nature and amusing antics (think early morning sunbathing and playing chicken with scorpions) have made meerkats extremely popular as subjects of wildlife documentaries. Our two habituated colonies have accepted the occasional presence of humans without letting it disrupt the important business of grooming and foraging.

Meerkats are just one of the smaller predators which abound at Tswalu: aardwolf, African wild cat and caracal are also frequently seen.
